## Audience And Communication Goal

The audience may not have a materials-science background. The poster must communicate three levels of understanding:

1. In approximately 15 seconds, a viewer should understand that soft fibers may harvest body heat for wearable electronics.
2. In 1-2 minutes, a viewer should follow the mechanism, fabrication flow, evidence, and practical potential.
3. In a deeper discussion, the presenter should be able to explain the experimental materials, measurement setup, and source paper.

Use short sentences, clear captions, and limited jargon. Keep necessary terminology such as `CNT`, `wet-spinning`, `Seebeck effect`, `PVA`, and `WPU`, but explain each term in context.

## Narrative Structure

### Hero Hook

Primary title:

> WHAT IF YOUR CLOTHES COULD HARVEST BODY HEAT?

Supporting line:

> Stretchable CNT/polymer fibers can convert temperature differences into small but stable electrical outputs while bending and stretching with the body.

The hero area should immediately connect body heat, stretchable fibers, and wearable power.

### Why It Matters

Explain:

- Wearable devices still depend on rigid batteries that require charging.
- Human body heat is a continuous energy source.
- A wearable generator must remain functional while the body bends, stretches, and moves.

### How It Works

Present a simple four-step visual flow:

`Body heat -> Temperature difference -> Seebeck effect -> Electrical voltage`

Add a one-sentence explanation:

> When the two ends of the fiber are at different temperatures, charge carriers move and generate a measurable voltage.

### How The Fiber Is Made

Use the wet-spinning figure from the paper with a concise four-step flow:

1. Prepare a stable CNT dispersion in water with SDS.
2. Extrude the dispersion into a WPU/PVA coagulation bath.
3. Allow polymer chains to infiltrate the CNT network.
4. Wash, dry, and anneal the fiber to improve its structure and performance.

### Key Evidence From The Published Study

Feature three large evidence callouts:

- `44.0 μV/K` stable Seebeck coefficient after annealing.
- `~30%` tensile strain while maintaining thermoelectric performance.
- `0.66 mV` stable output from a wearable device attached to skin.

Supporting note:

> The fiber maintains a stable voltage output while bending and stretching, which is essential for wearable applications.

These values must be clearly labelled as results reported by Zhang et al. (2021), not results produced by the presenter.

### Applications And Future Potential

Show near-term applications first:

- Heat-harvesting patches.
- Smart clothing.
- Low-power health sensors.

Show future potential in a quieter secondary strip:

- Sports wearables.
- Self-powered monitoring.
- Soft wearable electronics that reduce dependence on frequent charging.

Avoid claiming that the current prototype can fully power a smartwatch or other high-power consumer device. The source paper demonstrates small but stable electrical output and a path toward practical wearable integration.

### Our Experimental Proposal

Clearly label this section as a proposed reproduction workflow.

Core materials:

- Carboxylic SWNTs.
- Water and SDS.
- WPU and PVA coagulation bath.
- Conductive silver paste.

Core equipment:

- Probe ultrasonicator.
- Syringe pump.
- Vacuum oven at approximately `130°C`.
- Copper plates, PI heater, thermocouples, and nanovoltmeter.

Proposed workflow:

`Disperse -> Wet-spin -> Infiltrate -> Wash and anneal -> Measure voltage`
